## 1. First, a crucial issue with the proposed (sp^3) density

For four **real**, mutually orthonormal hybrids formed from

[
\boldsymbol\chi=(s,p_x,p_y,p_z),
]

you have

[
h_a=\sum_{\mu=0}^{3}U_{a\mu}\chi_\mu,
\qquad U\in O(4).
]

The number of degrees of freedom is not (10), but

[
16-\underbrace{4}*{\text{normalizations}}
-\underbrace{6}*{\text{pairwise orthogonality}}
=6.
]

Equivalently,

[
\dim O(4)=\frac{4(4-1)}2=6.
]

Three of these six parameters rotate the (p_x,p_y,p_z) subspace in physical space; the other three mix (s) with the (p) subspace and therefore change the hybridization.

For ideal tetrahedral hybrids,

[
h_a=
\frac12s+\frac{\sqrt3}{2},\mathbf n_a\cdot\mathbf p,
]

where the four tetrahedral directions satisfy

[
\mathbf n_a\cdot\mathbf n_b=-\frac13,
\qquad a\neq b.
]

Then

[
\langle h_a|h_b\rangle
======================

\frac14+\frac34\mathbf n_a\cdot\mathbf n_b
=\delta_{ab}.
]

However, there is a serious consequence:

[
\boxed{
\sum_{a=1}^{4}|h_a(\mathbf r)|^2
================================

|s(\mathbf r)|^2+
|p_x(\mathbf r)|^2+
|p_y(\mathbf r)|^2+
|p_z(\mathbf r)|^2
}
]

for **any** orthogonal (U).

If the three (p) orbitals share the same radial function,

[
|p_x|^2+|p_y|^2+|p_z|^2
=======================

R_p(r)^2
\frac{x^2+y^2+z^2}{r^2}
=======================

R_p(r)^2,
]

so the total incoherent density is spherical.

Therefore:

> Four equally occupied orthonormal hybrids, summed incoherently, do not generate a tetrahedral density at all.

This is basis invariance. Rotating occupied orbitals among themselves cannot change the total density.

Directional bond density in QM comes predominantly from **off-diagonal interatomic density-matrix terms**

[
\rho_{ij}(\mathbf r)
\sim
P_{ij}\phi_i(\mathbf r)\phi_j(\mathbf r),
]

not from the sum of isolated atomic orbital densities alone.

### How your (sp^3) model can still work

You should treat the four hybrids as **four saturable bonding ports**, rather than summing them into one atomic density.

Each neighbour interacts with one specific port:

[
E^\sigma_{ij}
=============

-D_\sigma f_\sigma(r_{ij})
A_{ia}(\hat{\mathbf r}*{ij})
A*{jb}(-\hat{\mathbf r}_{ij}),
]

where, for example,

[
A_{ia}=
\left[
\max\left(0,\mathbf n_{ia}\cdot\hat{\mathbf r}_{ij}\right)
\right]^m.
]

Then impose that each port can be assigned to at most one bond. In that case, the tetrahedral orientation matters even though the total isolated density is spherical.

For a first implementation I would not use a general (O(4)) matrix. I would use:

[
\mathbf n_{ia}=R(\mathbf q_i)\mathbf n_a^0,
]

where (\mathbf q_i) is one quaternion and (\mathbf n_a^0) are the four fixed tetrahedral directions. That gives only three orientational degrees of freedom and avoids many chemically strange (O(4)) transformations.

Later, three additional internal variables could control (s/p) mixing and allow (sp^3\leftrightarrow sp^2\leftrightarrow sp) rehybridization.

# 2. Alternating (\pi) bonds are exactly a local matching constraint

What you describe for the chain

[
A-B-C-D
]

is mathematically a **matching**, or in statistical mechanics a **dimer covering**.

Introduce a symmetric binary variable on every possible (\pi) bond,

[
d_{ij}=d_{ji}\in{0,1}.
]

Then impose the local constraint

[
\boxed{
\sum_{j\in N(i)}d_{ij}\leq 1
}
]

for every atom carrying one available (p) electron.

This immediately gives the desired result. If

[
d_{AB}=1,\qquad d_{CD}=1,
]

then at atoms (B) and (C),

[
d_{AB}+d_{BC}\leq1,
\qquad
d_{BC}+d_{CD}\leq1,
]

and therefore

[
d_{BC}=0.
]

A Kekulé structure is precisely a perfect matching of the conjugated carbon graph: every carbon is incident to exactly one selected (\pi)-bond edge. Classical and quantum dimer models use exactly the rule “one and only one dimer touching each site.” ([amu.hal.science][1])

So the algebraic construction you are looking for is not primarily a pseudo-spin. It is:

[
\boxed{\text{one local }\pi\text{-valence budget per atom}}
]

shared competitively between neighbouring bonds.

# 3. Continuous version suitable for molecular dynamics

Binary bond variables would produce discontinuous forces. Instead use

[
0\leq d_{ij}\leq1
]

and define the local (\pi)-valence sum

[
s_i=\sum_j d_{ij}.
]

A simple energy is

[
E_\pi
=====

-\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}J_{ij}d_{ij}
+
\frac{U}{2}\sum_i(s_i-q_i)^2
+
K\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}
d_{ij}^2(1-d_{ij})^2.
]

Here:

* (J_{ij}) is the geometry-dependent (\pi)-bond strength.
* (q_i=1) for a neutral (sp^2) carbon with one (\pi) electron available for pairing.
* (U) enforces the local electron budget.
* (K) makes (d_{ij}=0) and (1) preferable to fractional values.

For radicals and odd chains, introduce an unpaired-electron variable (m_i):

[
\boxed{
m_i+\sum_jd_{ij}=q_i
}
]

with

[
0\leq m_i\leq1,
]

and add a radical energy

[
E_{\rm radical}=\Delta_{\rm radical}\sum_i m_i.
]

Then an odd chain naturally contains one monomer/unpaired electron rather than violating the constraint.

This model captures:

* alternating double bonds;
* bond saturation;
* radicals and chain-end states;
* Kekulé defects;
* competition between several candidate (\pi) bonds;
* spontaneous switching between equivalent Kekulé patterns.

# 4. An especially simple “mutual choice” formulation

There is an even simpler representation that may suit your GPU/data-oriented style.

For every directed neighbour pair define

[
a_{i\rightarrow j}\geq0
]

with the atom-local normalization

[
\boxed{
m_i+\sum_j a_{i\rightarrow j}=1
}.
]

Interpret (a_{i\rightarrow j}) as the fraction of atom (i)'s (p) electron offered to neighbour (j).

A (\pi) bond forms only when both atoms select each other:

[
b_{ij}
======

a_{i\rightarrow j}a_{j\rightarrow i}.
]

Then

[
E_\pi=-\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}J_{ij}
a_{i\rightarrow j}a_{j\rightarrow i}
+
\Delta_{\rm radical}\sum_i m_i.
]

This automatically gives bond competition.

For the chain (A-B-C-D), the lowest-energy configuration for equal strong couplings is

[
a_{A\rightarrow B}=a_{B\rightarrow A}=1,
]

[
a_{C\rightarrow D}=a_{D\rightarrow C}=1,
]

and

[
a_{B\rightarrow C}=a_{C\rightarrow B}=0.
]

Atom (B) cannot fully select both (A) and (C), because

[
a_{B\rightarrow A}+a_{B\rightarrow C}\leq1.
]

That is the local analogue of Pauli/valence saturation you need.

A smooth winner-takes-all update could be

[
a_{i\rightarrow j}^{\rm new}
============================

\frac{
\exp\left[\beta J_{ij}a_{j\rightarrow i}\right]
}{
\exp(-\beta\Delta_{\rm radical})
+
\sum_k
\exp\left[\beta J_{ik}a_{k\rightarrow i}\right]
}.
]

The radical occupancy is

[
m_i^{\rm new}
=============

\frac{
\exp(-\beta\Delta_{\rm radical})
}{
\exp(-\beta\Delta_{\rm radical})
+
\sum_k
\exp\left[\beta J_{ik}a_{k\rightarrow i}\right]
}.
]

At small (\beta), the bond order spreads over neighbours, resembling delocalized conjugation. At large (\beta), it becomes winner-takes-all and approaches a Kekulé matching.

Use damping,

[
a^{n+1}
=======

(1-\gamma)a^n+\gamma a^{\rm new},
]

because an undamped synchronous update can oscillate between competing partners.

This algorithm is entirely local:

1. each bond computes (J_{ij});
2. each atom gathers neighbour offers;
3. each atom normalizes its outgoing allocations;
4. bond orders are products of the two directed allocations.

No diagonalization, global linear solve, or atomics are required.

It is not guaranteed to produce the exact maximum-weight matching on every arbitrary graph. Exact matching may require information propagated along long alternating paths. But repeated local updates can approximate it well, and for chains, rings and mostly three-coordinated carbon networks it is a very natural starting point.

# 5. The (p)-orbital orientation vector is useful—but it does not enforce exclusion

Let (\mathbf h_i) be the axis of the (p) orbital on atom (i).

A (p) orbital is physically an **axis/director**, not an arrow:

[
\mathbf h_i\equiv-\mathbf h_i.
]

Changing the sign of one basis orbital is only a gauge change. Therefore a rule such as

[
\hat{\mathbf r}_{ij}\cdot(\mathbf h_i-\mathbf h_j)>0
]

is problematic: flipping the arbitrary sign of (\mathbf h_i) changes whether the bond is classified as bonding.

A better sign-invariant geometrical overlap is obtained by projecting both orbitals perpendicular to the bond:

[
\mathbf h_i^\perp
=================

\mathbf h_i-
(\mathbf h_i\cdot\hat{\mathbf r}*{ij})\hat{\mathbf r}*{ij},
]

and similarly for (j). Then define

[
G_{ij}^\pi
==========

\left(
\mathbf h_i^\perp\cdot\mathbf h_j^\perp
\right)^2.
]

Equivalently,

[
G_{ij}^\pi
==========

\left[
\mathbf h_i\cdot\mathbf h_j
---------------------------

(\mathbf h_i\cdot\hat{\mathbf r}*{ij})
(\mathbf h_j\cdot\hat{\mathbf r}*{ij})
\right]^2.
]

The available (\pi)-bond strength can be

[
J_{ij}
======

D_\pi f_\pi(r_{ij})G_{ij}^\pi.
]

This correctly rewards:

* both (p) orbitals being perpendicular to the bond;
* the two orbital axes being parallel;
* coplanar (sp^2) geometry.

But (G_{ij}^\pi) only says **how favourable** a bond would be. It does not prevent one atom from bonding to two neighbours. You still need the allocation or dimer constraint.

Thus the clean separation is

[
\boxed{
\text{orbital orientation}
\quad+\quad
\text{local electron/bond capacity}
}.
]

# 6. Why pseudo-charge (+,-,+,-) is insufficient

A staggered pseudo-charge

[
\sigma_i=\pm1
]

can identify the two sublattices of a bipartite conjugated network. You could allow a bond only when

[
\sigma_i\sigma_j=-1.
]

But in a chain

[
+,-,+,-
]

every nearest-neighbour pair has opposite signs. Therefore it permits

[
AB,\quad BC,\quad CD
]

simultaneously.

It encodes **compatibility**, not **saturation**.

Likewise, an antiferromagnetic spin model can enforce alternating phases but does not stop one spin from interacting with all its neighbours.

To model one electron participating in one singlet valence bond, the essential object is an occupancy constraint,

[
\sum_jd_{ij}\leq1,
]

not merely an alternating sign.

# 7. Bonding versus antibonding cannot be represented just by pointing lobes

For two (p) orbitals, the bonding and antibonding combinations are

[
\psi_+
======

\frac{\phi_i+\phi_j}{\sqrt2},
\qquad
\psi_-
======

\frac{\phi_i-\phi_j}{\sqrt2}.
]

But replacing (\phi_i\rightarrow-\phi_i) swaps the labels (+) and (-). Therefore the signs of isolated atomic orbitals have no absolute physical meaning.

What matters is:

* the hopping matrix element;
* which resulting state is occupied;
* the global consistency of phases around loops.

A local vector orientation can model the magnitude of overlap, but it cannot by itself decide whether the bonding or antibonding molecular state is occupied.

Your dimer/allocation variable is therefore better interpreted as an approximate **local bond-order density matrix element**, rather than as the literal sign of an atomic orbital.

# 8. Relation to existing forcefields

Tersoff/REBO-type bond-order potentials already use local coordination and angular environment to weaken bonds as an atom acquires more neighbours. The second-generation REBO potential includes separate (\sigma)-(\pi), dihedral and radical/conjugation corrections, and explicitly aims to account for conjugation without diagonalizing an electronic Hamiltonian. ([Physics User Site][2])

REBO's conjugation term is still largely an empirical local coordination descriptor; it does not explicitly construct a Kekulé matching. Your explicit bond-budget variables would make the valence-bond saturation much more transparent.

A more systematic intermediate level is an analytic bond-order potential derived from moments of a tight-binding density of states. The moments correspond to closed hopping paths of increasing length, so progressively longer-range conjugation information can be included without global diagonalization. ([juser.fz-juelich.de][3])

A reasonable hierarchy is therefore:

1. **Local dimer/allocation model**
   Cheapest; explicit bond saturation and Kekulé patterns.

2. **REBO-like local conjugation corrections**
   Add coordination, dihedral and radical energetics.

3. **Finite local path/moment corrections**
   Add ring and longer conjugation effects without solving global orbitals.

# 9. What your fully local model can and cannot reproduce

It can plausibly capture:

* (sp^3), (sp^2) and possibly (sp) local geometry;
* bond saturation;
* alternating double bonds;
* radicals and domain walls;
* torsional weakening of (\pi) bonds;
* Peierls/Kekulé symmetry breaking;
* bond switching during reactions.

It will not automatically reproduce:

* aromatic stabilization of benzene relative to a localized cyclohexatriene;
* the (4n+2) rule;
* antiaromatic (4n) destabilization;
* global phase interference around rings;
* complete fermionic nodal structure;
* molecular orbital shell filling.

Those are genuinely nonlocal consequences of coherent electron propagation and Fermi statistics.

For benzene, for example, the strict classical matching model chooses one of two Kekulé patterns. The actual ground state gains energy from resonance between both. You can approximate that either by:

* allowing fractional bonds (d_{ij}=1/2);
* thermally/dynamically switching between the two coverings;
* adding a local six-membered-ring resonance term;
* or including short closed-path corrections.

## My recommended minimal prototype

Use:

[
\boxed{
\text{fixed tetrahedral }\sigma\text{ ports}
+
\text{one }p\text{-axis on }sp^2\text{ atoms}
+
\text{one local }\pi\text{-electron budget}
}
]

with

[
J_{ij}
======

D_\pi f_\pi(r_{ij})
\left[
\mathbf h_i\cdot\mathbf h_j
---------------------------

(\mathbf h_i\cdot\hat{\mathbf r}*{ij})
(\mathbf h_j\cdot\hat{\mathbf r}*{ij})
\right]^2,
]

[
m_i+\sum_j a_{i\rightarrow j}=1,
]

[
b_{ij}=a_{i\rightarrow j}a_{j\rightarrow i},
]

[
E_\pi=-\sum_{ij}J_{ij}b_{ij}
+\Delta_{\rm radical}\sum_i m_i.
]

That is probably the simplest genuinely local classical construction containing:

* orbital directionality;
* bond mutuality;
* valence saturation;
* alternating conjugated bonds;
* radicals;
* continuous differentiable bond order.

The essential insight is that **nodal orientation and Pauli saturation are two different variables**. The 3D orbital director handles geometry; the neighbour-space allocation handles exclusion.
