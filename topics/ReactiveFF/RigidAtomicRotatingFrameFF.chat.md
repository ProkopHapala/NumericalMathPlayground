# 2. Conventional atomistic force fields

UFF, MMFF, AMBER/GAFF, CHARMM and related force fields normally use nuclear Cartesian positions as their only configurational variables. Their valence energy is decomposed into explicit bond, angle, torsion and improper terms:

[
E_{\mathrm{val}}
================

\sum_b E_b(r_b)
+
\sum_a E_a(\theta_a)
+
\sum_t E_t(\phi_t)
+
\sum_{\mathrm{imp}}E_{\mathrm{imp}}.
]

The angle and torsional parameters are fitted explicitly; there is no independent atomic orientation or angular velocity. This remains true even when the force field contains many cross terms or sophisticated angular functional forms. ([American Chemical Society Publications][1])

Your representation replaces much of

[
{\theta_{jik}}_{j,k\in N(i)}
]

by one local rotation (R_i). For coordination (z_i), a conventional force field can have

[
\frac{z_i(z_i-1)}2
]

angle terms around atom (i), whereas your atom has (z_i) ports and one three-dimensional orientation.

That is a real structural difference, not simply another angular potential.

# 3. The closest exact precedent: patchy particles

The kinematics of your atoms is essentially identical to that of a **patchy particle**:

* a center position;
* a rigid-body orientation;
* fixed patches or arms in the body frame;
* an excluded-volume core;
* directional interactions through the patches.

The Kern–Frenkel model introduced hard-sphere particles with short-range orientation-dependent attractive patches. Later tetrahedral variants used four patches fixed on a sphere to generate diamond-like coordination. ([AIP Publishing][2])

The resemblance is especially strong to the three-dimensional Mercedes-Benz water model. Each water object is represented as a Lennard–Jones sphere carrying four tetrahedrally arranged body-frame arms; hydrogen bonding depends on the mutual alignment of those arms and the intermolecular axis. 

Modern patchy-particle simulations routinely represent particle orientation by normalized quaternions and rotate body-frame patch directions into the world frame. ([American Chemical Society Publications][3])

Therefore you should **not** claim that “particles with quaternions and fixed directional ports” are new.

The distinction is:

> Patchy-particle models normally use these objects as coarse-grained molecules, proteins, colloids or abstract associating particles. You propose to use the same orientational representation at the level of each chemical atom and to replace conventional covalent angle machinery.

That atomistic use is the potentially novel part.

# 4. VALBOND and orbital-based angular force fields

VALBOND is probably the chemically closest established force-field family.

VALBOND derives molecular-mechanics angular energies from valence-bond and hybrid-orbital concepts. It evaluates orbital-strength and nonorthogonality functions and has been used for unusual coordination geometries, hypervalent compounds and transition-metal complexes. In VALBOND–CHARMM implementations, conventional angle terms can be replaced by hybrid-orbital strength functions. ([American Chemical Society Publications][4])

Multi-State VALBOND extends this framework to different bonding or coordination states and reactive processes. ([American Chemical Society Publications][5])

The crucial difference is that VALBOND still computes its energy from the **instantaneous nuclear bond vectors and angles**. Its hybrid orbitals are conceptual or algebraic objects; it does not normally propagate one independent quaternion and angular momentum for every atom.

Thus VALBOND supports your physical motivation:

[
\text{local hybrid orbitals}
\longrightarrow
\text{preferred coordination geometry},
]

but it does not appear to use your dynamical representation:

[
(x_i,q_i,v_i,\omega_i).
]

There is also an older literature called the **Hybrid Orbital Force Field**, or HOFF, used particularly in vibrational force-constant analysis. That literature introduced relationships between force constants based on hybrid-orbital models, but it was not an oriented-atom molecular-dynamics formulation of the kind you are developing. ([American Chemical Society Publications][6])

# 5. Local atomic frames, multipoles and virtual sites

AMOEBA and related polarizable multipole force fields define a local coordinate frame at each atomic site. Atomic multipoles are stored in this frame and transformed as molecular geometry changes. ([American Chemical Society Publications][7])

Off-center charges, lone-pair sites, sigma-hole sites and anisotropic virtual-site models also use local frames to place auxiliary interaction centers around an atom. These models improve directional electrostatics and hydrogen or halogen bonding. ([American Chemical Society Publications][8])

But in these force fields the local frame is usually **constructed from neighboring nuclear positions**:

[
R_i=R_i(x_j,x_k,\ldots).
]

It is not an independent dynamical variable:

[
R_i\ne R_i(x_j,x_k,\ldots)
\quad\text{in your model}.
]

This distinction is significant. Your frame can lag, rotate, carry angular momentum and act as a compact auxiliary representation of the local electronic/bonding geometry.

# 6. Directional and reactive interatomic potentials

Stillinger–Weber, Tersoff, REBO, EDIP, ReaxFF and similar potentials already represent covalent directionality without conventional fixed bond topology.

For example:

* Stillinger–Weber uses explicit three-body angular terms to favor tetrahedral coordination.
* Tersoff and REBO use environment-dependent bond order with angular dependence.
* ReaxFF uses distance-dependent bond orders together with valence-angle and torsional contributions. ([AIP Publishing][9])

These models demonstrate that local coordination can be encoded through many-body environment dependence. But they still use only atomic positions. They do not introduce an independent local (SO(3)) frame for each atom.

Your model could be viewed as a **factorization or low-rank representation of a many-body angular potential**.

# 7. Mathematical relation to an ordinary angle force field

This may be the strongest theoretical part of a paper.

For a central atom (i), define

[
E_i(x,R_i)
==========

\frac12
\sum_{\alpha}
k_{\alpha}
\left|
d_\alpha-R_i a_\alpha
\right|^2,
\qquad
d_\alpha=x_{j_\alpha}-x_i.
]

If the orientation is adiabatically eliminated,

[
\boxed{
E_i^{\mathrm{eff}}({d_\alpha})
==============================

\min_{R_i\in SO(3)}
E_i({d_\alpha},R_i),
}
]

then this is exactly an ARAP or weighted shape-matching energy applied to the coordination shell.

It generates an effective many-body angular potential among all neighbor vectors (d_\alpha), even though the implementation contains only atom-to-port residuals.

Near equilibrium, write

[
R_i\simeq R_i^0\exp([\delta\theta_i]_\times),
]

and

[
d_\alpha=d_\alpha^0+\delta d_\alpha.
]

Then

[
E_i\simeq
\frac12\sum_\alpha k_\alpha
\left|
\delta d_\alpha
---------------

\delta\theta_i\times r_\alpha^0
\right|^2.
]

Eliminating (\delta\theta_i) gives a quadratic energy in all neighbor displacements:

[
E_i^{\mathrm{eff}}
==================

\frac12\delta d^T
\left[
K-KB(B^TKB)^{-1}B^TK
\right]
\delta d.
]

This shows that your representation is not merely “four independent springs.” The shared atomic rotation creates correlated angular stiffness between all ports.

That provides several useful interpretations:

* a low-rank factorization of an angle–angle Hessian;
* an atom-centered ARAP model;
* a discrete micropolar or Cosserat-like model;
* a classical localized-hybrid-orbital model.

# 8. Advantages that could be publishable

## Linear rather than quadratic local topology

For an atom with (z) neighbors:

* conventional angles: (O(z^2)) angle pairs;
* port representation: (O(z)) port terms plus one local orientation.

For ordinary organic atoms with (z\le4), the asymptotic difference is not enormous, but the simpler memory access and regular atom-centered GPU kernel may still be important.

## Natural correlated angle motion

Conventional force fields often treat each angle independently. Your shared local frame couples all bond directions automatically and makes the coordination shell move approximately as one rigid polyhedron.

## Natural stereochemistry

A labeled ordered set of tetrahedral ports can encode chirality directly. Inversion of a stereocenter cannot occur without a large rearrangement or an explicit change of port assignment.

## Natural lone pairs and directional chemistry

Unused ports can represent:

* lone pairs;
* hydrogen-bond acceptor directions;
* sigma holes;
* π-orbital normals;
* coordination vacancies.

## Simple force and torque evaluation

Each port produces one force at an offset point:

[
F_{i\alpha},\qquad
\tau_{i\alpha}=r_{i\alpha}\times F_{i\alpha}.
]

This gives a compact Newton–Euler formulation and fits your atom-centered gather architecture.

## Flexible solver choice

The same representation can be used with:

* local orientation minimization;
* Projective Dynamics;
* XPBD;
* soft sequential impulses;
* VBD/AVBD;
* damped molecular relaxation.

The representation is therefore separable from the numerical solver.

# 9. Important physical issue: these are auxiliary electronic degrees of freedom

A real classical point nucleus has only three configurational coordinates. Adding (q_i) introduces three additional coordinates per atom.

Those rotations should not generally be interpreted as literal mechanical rotation of the atomic nucleus. They represent a local valence-electron or hybridization frame.

This has an important consequence.

If you give every quaternion an ordinary inertia, place it at the same temperature as nuclear motion and perform equilibrium MD, equipartition will assign thermal energy to these additional modes. You would obtain extra heat capacity, extra spectral modes and possibly incorrect equilibrium statistics.

There are three defensible regimes.

### Adiabatic orientation

At each nuclear configuration, minimize

[
R_i^\star
=========

\arg\min_{R_i} E.
]

Then the physical potential is

[
E_{\mathrm{BO}}(x)=E(x,R^\star(x)).
]

The orientations are auxiliary variables but not thermodynamic degrees of freedom. This is the cleanest interpretation for a classical force field.

### Extended-Lagrangian orientation

Assign a small fictitious inertia and propagate (q_i) close to its instantaneous optimum, but keep the orientational subsystem cold or strongly damped.

This is analogous to classical Drude polarization, where auxiliary electronic variables can either be minimized self-consistently or propagated with fictitious masses and a separate low-temperature thermostat. ([American Chemical Society Publications][10])

### Pure structural relaxation

For fast minimization rather than equilibrium MD, the fictitious inertia and damping are simply numerical preconditioning parameters. In that context there is no problem with additional thermodynamic degrees of freedom.

This distinction should be explicit in a publication.

# 10. Limitations that must be addressed

## The local port polyhedron may be too rigid

One quaternion rotates all ports together. It cannot independently change the six angles of an (sp^3) coordination shell.

This is an advantage if you want a rigid tetrahedron, but a limitation when different angular deformation modes have significantly different stiffnesses.

Possible extensions are:

[
a_{i\alpha}=a_{i\alpha}^0+\sum_\mu c_\mu b_{i\alpha}^{(\mu)},
]

where (c_\mu) are one or two internal shape modes in addition to the quaternion.

## Hybridization changes

A fixed port set cannot smoothly represent

[
sp \leftrightarrow sp^2 \leftrightarrow sp^3
]

without extra machinery. You may need:

* discrete atomic states;
* multiple port templates;
* state interpolation;
* bond-order-dependent port geometry.

## Torsions are not automatically solved

Ports fix local coordination, but an isolated sigma bond can still allow rotation of one atomic frame relative to the other.

You may require an explicit frame–frame torsional term such as

[
E_{\mathrm{tors}}
=================

\sum_n k_n
\left[
1-\cos(n\phi-\phi_0)
\right],
]

or interactions between additional π/lone-pair ports.

## Symmetry and gauge

For four identical tetrahedral ports, several rotations represent the same physical port set. Quaternion space therefore contains symmetry-equivalent minima. For planar or linear environments there may also be an unconstrained rotation about the symmetry axis.

These are manageable, but they should be treated deliberately.
